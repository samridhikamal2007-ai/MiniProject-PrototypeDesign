1. Project Overview

LearnSphere is a mobile-first online learning platform concept designed to make skill-building feel approachable, motivating, and friction-free. The prototype demonstrates a complete learner journey — from first launch and course discovery, through a detailed course page and video lesson, into a personal learning dashboard and profile. Every screen is interconnected with working navigation so stakeholders can click through the experience exactly as a real user would.

The product targets self-directed learners — students, early-career professionals, and career-switchers — who want short, structured, expert-led courses they can complete on the go. Rather than replicating the dense, course-catalog feel of legacy platforms, LearnSphere prioritises a calm visual hierarchy, generous spacing, and clear primary actions so users always know what to do next.

2. Problem Statement & Goals

The problem

Existing learning apps overwhelm new users with huge catalogs, dense course pages, and unclear progress feedback. Learners drop off because they cannot quickly find a relevant course, do not feel a sense of momentum, and lose track of where they left off.

Design goals

  • Reduce time-to-first-course to under 60 seconds
  • Make progress visible and motivating on every visit
  • Keep course detail pages scannable, not intimidating
  • Support one-thumb mobile navigation throughout

3. Design Thinking Process

The prototype was built following the five-stage Stanford d.school design-thinking framework, ensuring decisions were grounded in user needs rather than aesthetics alone.

1 · Empathise→ 2 · Define→ 3 · Ideate→ 4 · Prototype→ 5 · Test

Empathise

Researched pain points of popular apps (Udemy, Coursera) through reviews and forums: catalog overload, unclear progress, clunky video players.

Define

Synthesised findings into two primary personas and a core user need: “Help me find a relevant course fast and show me I'm progressing.”

Ideate

Sketched 6 core screens, prioritising a “continue learning” entry point and a single sticky enrol/CTA per page.

6. Design System

A token-based design system keeps the product visually consistent and easy to scale. Colours, typography, spacing, and components were defined once and reused across all six screens — mirroring how a Figma library with styles and components would be structured.

6.1 Colour Palette

The palette pairs a trustworthy indigo primary with a warm coral accent. Indigo conveys focus and learning; coral draws the eye to the single most important action on each screen. Neutrals carry content without competing.

6.3 Spacing, Radius & Components

Spacing scale (4pt base)

4 · 8 · 12 · 16 · 20 · 24 · 32 — applied as consistent padding rhythm. Screen edge padding = 20px; card padding = 14–16px; section gaps = 26px.

Radius & elevation

Radii: 10 / 16 / 22 / 30 / pill. Three shadow tiers (sm, md, lg) plus a coloured primary glow for the main CTA to signal interactivity.

Reusable components include: pill buttons (primary / accent / outline / ghost), category chips, course cards (small grid + large featured), progress bars and rings, rating stars, the bottom tab bar, and a sticky purchase/CTA bar. Building these once as variants mirrors a component library in Figma and guarantees pixel consistency.

7. Information Architecture & User Flow

The information architecture is intentionally shallow — three taps maximum to any core action — to suit mobile attention spans. The primary flow below was the spine of the prototype.

Onboarding→ Home / Discovery→ Course Detail→ Enroll→ Video Lesson→ Mark Complete→ Dashboard

A persistent bottom tab bar (Home, Explore, My Learning, Profile) lets users jump between the discovery and progress halves of the app at any point, supporting both linear and exploratory navigation styles identified during research.

9. Usability Principles Applied

Jakob's Law — familiarity

Bottom tab bar, search position, and progress patterns match conventions users already know, lowering the learning curve.

Hick's Law — fewer choices

Category chips are horizontally scrollable but only one row; each screen surfaces a single primary action to reduce decision load.

Fitts's Law — thumb reach

Primary CTAs sit in the bottom sticky bar within the thumb zone; tap targets meet the 44pt minimum throughout.

Visibility of progress

A weekly-goal ring, per-course bars, streaks, and badges keep momentum visible — directly addressing the #1 research pain point.

Screen-by-screen rationale

  • Onboarding — A single hero illustration, a three-dot progress indicator, and a clearly labelled primary CTA. “Skip” respects users who want to browse before committing.
  • Home / Discovery — Opens with a personalised “Continue Learning” hero so returning users resume in one tap. Category chips and a 2-up course grid support fast scanning; a featured card surfaces a top pick.
  • Course Detail — A gradient video-preview header, scannable tabs (Overview / Curriculum / Reviews), an instructor card for trust, and a sticky bottom bar carrying price + enrol so the CTA never scrolls out of reach.
  • Video Lesson — Dark video stage for focus, inline timeline and speed control, contextual actions (helpful / notes / discuss), a pro-tip callout, and an “Up Next” list to reduce drop-off between lessons.
  • My Learning Dashboard — At-a-glance stats, a weekly-goal ring, in-progress courses with progress bars, a 7-day activity chart, and achievement badges — all engineered to sustain motivation.
  • Profile — Cover + avatar identity, certificate downloads (a key motivator for career-switchers), and grouped settings menus following iOS/Android conventions for instant familiarity.

11. Usability Testing Plan

Because this is a concept prototype, a lightweight moderated testing plan was prepared to validate the design before development. Five participants matching the two personas would complete three core tasks while thinking aloud.

#TaskSuccess metricWhat we'd learn
1Find and open a Design course≤ 30s, no back-trackingDiscovery clarity, chip usability
2Enrol and start the first lessonCompletes flow, notices CTASticky CTA effectiveness
3Return to an in-progress courseUses dashboard in ≤ 2 tapsResume path & progress visibility

Metrics captured: task success rate, time-on-task, number of taps, and System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaire. Predicted issues to watch: category chip discoverability on small screens and whether the weekly-goal ring reads as motivating versus pressuring.

12. Accessibility Considerations

  • Body text meets WCAG AA contrast (ink on white ≈ 11.6:1)
  • All interactive elements ≥ 44×44 pt tap targets
  • Colour is never the sole indicator (icons + labels accompany states)
  • Focus order follows visual order; semantic headings structure content
  • Respect for motion: transitions are short and could be disabled
  • Dynamic Type-friendly sizing using relative units

13. Conclusion & Next Steps

The LearnSphere prototype demonstrates a complete, research-backed user journey for an online learning platform, built with a consistent design system and grounded in recognised usability principles. The clickable prototype lets stakeholders experience the flow end-to-end — from onboarding through to a certificate-ready profile — while this document makes the reasoning behind each decision transparent.

Recommended next steps

  • Run the 5-participant moderated test and iterate on chip discoverability
  • Add dark-mode tokens and empty/loading/error states to the system
  • Expand to a responsive web companion reusing the same component library
  • Introduce micro-interactions (button press, lesson-complete celebration) for delight
